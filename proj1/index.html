Single-Scale Plates

Color reconstruction from Prokudin-Gorskii plates can be reduced to a problem of identity across three appearances. Each plate supplies three grayscale exposures of one scene, taken through blue, green, and red filters, yet recorded at slightly different moments. The scene is therefore treated as the stable cause, and the three bands are treated as its varying effects. Alignment is justified when the same geometric structures—edges, contours, and repeated textures—recur in all three bands, though displaced by the motion of the camera or the subject between exposures.

The reconstruction pipeline can be decomposed into three transformations. First, the plate is split into three bands and the blue band is chosen as the reference frame. Second, each remaining band is shifted over a window of candidate translations, and a similarity score is computed on an interior region so that colored borders and scanner artifacts do not dominate the comparison. Third, the best shifts are applied, the valid overlap region is cropped to the common intersection, and the result is stacked in red–green–blue order to form a single RGB image. When the shifts are correct, structural features coincide; when they are wrong, colored fringes persist along high-contrast boundaries.

Other plates: raw triptychs

The two displays serve different epistemic roles. The Monastery belt isolates the three observations of a single cause and makes the need for translation explicit. The multi-plate belt exhibits variation in exposure, texture, and border artifacts, and therefore clarifies why the similarity score is restricted to an interior region and why structural features provide a more stable basis for agreement than raw brightness.
